Steven Pinker's Enlightenment Now: The Case for Reason, Science, and Humanism

Why one of the most controversial books of the 21st century is also one of the most important

In 2018, Harvard cognitive scientist Steven Pinker published Enlightenment Now: The Case for Reason, Science, Humanism, and Progress. The book landed like a grenade in a culture that had largely decided things were getting worse. It argued the opposite — not with platitudes, but with 75 charts covering 15 dimensions of human welfare over 200 years.

The reaction was fierce. Critics called it naive, complacent, and tone-deaf to suffering. Supporters called it the most important book of the decade. Both reactions miss what makes the book significant. Pinker's contribution isn't the conclusion that things are getting better — that's empirically settled. It's the explanation: why they got better, and what we'd lose if we abandoned the tools that did it.

The Core Argument

Pinker's thesis has three layers.

Layer 1: Things got better. Across every measurable dimension of human welfare — life expectancy, health, prosperity, safety, literacy, access to knowledge, political freedom — the trend lines point upward. A person born in 1820 had a 1 in 4 chance of dying before age 5. Today, globally, it's under 4%. A person born in 1820 could expect to live 30 years. Today, 73. These are not marginal improvements. They are transformations.

The wealth shift: In 1820, 89% of the world's population lived in extreme poverty (under $2/day in today's terms). Today, it's under 9%. That means roughly 2 billion people escaped poverty in the last two centuries — most of them in the last 50 years.

Layer 2: They got better for identifiable reasons. Pinker attributes progress to the Enlightenment's core values: reason, science, and humanism. Reason — the commitment to evaluating claims by evidence rather than authority or tradition. Science — the systematic pursuit of knowledge through observation and experiment. Humanism — the principle that human well-being is the ultimate moral concern, not the glory of a nation, religion, or leader.

These aren't abstractions. The Green Revolution — Norman Borlaug's dwarf wheat varieties that saved an estimated billion lives from famine — is science applied to humanism. The abolition of smallpox, which killed 300 million people in the 20th century alone, is the same combination. The expansion of democracy from 12 countries in 1943 to 96 today is reason applied to governance.

Layer 3: These values are not self-sustaining. This is the part critics miss. Pinker is not an optimist in the naive sense. He explicitly warns that progress is not inevitable. The Enlightenment values that drove progress are under attack from multiple directions — religious fundamentalism, nationalist populism, postmodern relativism, and what he calls "romantic environmentalism" (the idea that nature is pure and human intervention is corrupting). Each of these movements rejects at least one Enlightenment pillar, and each has real political momentum.

The 75 Charts

The empirical backbone of Enlightenment Now is 75 graphs, each tracking a dimension of human welfare over time. The data comes from sources like the World Bank, the UN, Our World in Data, and peer-reviewed research. Here's a sampling:

Health and Survival

Life expectancy has risen from 30 to 73 globally. Child mortality has fallen from 24% to under 4%. Maternal mortality has dropped 98% since 1900. The number of people killed by infectious diseases has fallen by roughly half in the last 20 years alone. Smallpox, polio, guinea worm disease, and rubella have been eliminated or nearly eliminated.

Wealth

Global GDP per capita has increased 100-fold since 1800. The percentage of people living in extreme poverty has dropped from 89% to under 9%. The global middle class is expanding faster than at any point in history — roughly 150,000 people join it every day.

The China example: In 1981, 88% of China's population lived in extreme poverty. By 2018, it was under 1%. That's 800 million people escaping poverty in a single country in a single generation — the fastest poverty reduction in human history.

Peace and Safety

Battle deaths have fallen from 22 per 100,000 in 1950 to under 1 today. Homicide rates in Western Europe have dropped 50-fold since the Middle Ages. Deaths from natural disasters have fallen 95% over the last century, despite population growth — because of better infrastructure, early warning systems, and emergency response.

Knowledge and Freedom

Global literacy has risen from 12% in 1820 to 86% today. The number of democratic countries has grown from 12 in 1943 to 96 in 2018. Access to electricity, clean water, and basic sanitation has expanded to cover the majority of humanity. The share of people who have never attended school has collapsed from 83% in 1820 to under 17% today.

The Critics — and Where They're Right

Not all criticism of Pinker is unfounded. The most serious objections come from three directions.

Inequality. Progress in averages can mask stagnation at the bottom. The global poverty line is $2.15/day — escaping it doesn't mean comfort. A person earning $3/day is still desperately poor by any humane standard. Pinker acknowledges this but argues that the solution is more progress, not less — the same Enlightenment tools that raised billions from $1 to $3 can raise them from $3 to $10.

Environmental degradation. Climate change is the gravest counterargument to the "things are getting better" thesis. Pinker treats this seriously, noting that CO2 emissions are the one trend line that points the wrong way. His response is characteristically Enlightenment: the same science that identified the problem can solve it. Solar energy costs have dropped 89% in the last decade. Nuclear, geothermal, and battery technologies are improving. The path exists; the question is political will.

Meaning and connection. Material progress doesn't guarantee psychological flourishing. Depression, anxiety, loneliness, and social fragmentation have risen in wealthy countries. Pinker's answer is that these are real problems but are themselves solvable — and that pre-industrial societies had their own versions, plus starvation, disease, and violence that we've largely eliminated.

Why the Book Matters Now

The cultural moment that made Enlightenment Now controversial has only intensified. Surveys consistently show that the majority of people in wealthy countries believe the world is getting worse — despite the data. The gap between perception and reality is itself a problem, because it drives bad policy. If you believe things are falling apart, you're susceptible to radical solutions — dismantling the institutions that drove progress in the name of "fixing" them.

Pinker's most important contribution may be the warning embedded in the book's structure. The 75 charts show what the Enlightenment delivered. The final chapters show what happens when we forget that delivery — when reason is dismissed as a tool of oppression, when science is treated as one opinion among many, when humanism is replaced by tribal identity. The 20th century already ran this experiment. The results were catastrophic.

The book's deepest argument is not "things are fine." It's: things got better for specific, identifiable reasons, and those reasons are not permanent. They are values, and values can be abandoned.
