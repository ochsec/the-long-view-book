Is the World Getting Better? The Data Says Yes

The statistics that prove human progress is real — despite what the news tells you

The question seems simple: is the world getting better or worse? Most people answer worse. They are wrong. The data on virtually every measure of human welfare points in one direction, and that direction is up. This is not a matter of interpretation or political perspective. It is a matter of measurement.

Steven Pinker made this case in The Better Angels of Our Nature (2011) and Enlightenment Now (2018), drawing on datasets from the World Bank, the UN, the WHO, and dozens of peer-reviewed studies. Hans Rosling made it in Factfulness (2017). Max Roser's Our World in Data project has made it continuously, updating the charts as new data arrives. The evidence is not hidden. It is publicly available, regularly updated, and remarkably consistent.

Here is the data, category by category.

Poverty: The Greatest Story Never Told

In 1820, approximately 94 percent of the world's population lived in extreme poverty — defined as living on less than $1.90 per day, adjusted for purchasing power. Today, that figure is under 9 percent. The decline happened in waves: slow through the 19th century, accelerating through the 20th, and dramatic since 1990. In 1990, 36 percent of the world's population lived in extreme poverty. By 2015, it was 10 percent. By 2023, it was under 9 percent.

94% → 9% Global extreme poverty rate, 1820 to today

That means roughly 137,000 people escape extreme poverty every single day — averaged over the last two decades. No newspaper reports this. No social media algorithm surfaces it. It is the most important ongoing story on earth, and it is invisible in the information environment you live in.

The progress is not evenly distributed. Sub-Saharan Africa lags behind. Within countries, inequality has widened even as absolute poverty has fallen. These qualifications matter — but they do not change the direction of the trend. The world is poorer than it should be, but it is dramatically less poor than it was.

Health: Longer Lives, Fewer Deaths

In 1900, the average global life expectancy was 32 years. Today, it is 73. That is not a rounding error — it is a doubling of the human lifespan in just over a century. The improvement happened everywhere, including in the poorest countries. A child born in India today has a longer expected life than a child born in the United States in 1900.

32 → 73 Average global life expectancy, 1900 to today

Child mortality tells an even more dramatic story. In 1800, approximately 43 percent of children worldwide died before their fifth birthday. Today, that figure is under 4 percent. In 1950, it was still 22 percent. The decline from 22 percent to under 4 percent happened in a single lifetime — your lifetime, if you are old enough to read this.

Smallpox, which killed an estimated 300 million people in the 20th century alone, was eradicated in 1980. Polio is on the verge of eradication, with cases down 99 percent since 1988. Measles deaths fell by 78 percent between 2000 and 2023. These are not projections or hopes — they are measured outcomes.

Violence: The Long Decline

This is the category that surprises people most. The belief that violence is increasing is one of the most widely held and most provably wrong beliefs about the world.

In pre-state societies, the archaeological and anthropological evidence suggests that approximately 15 percent of people died by violence. In the 20th century — including two world wars, the Holocaust, Stalin's purges, Mao's Great Leap Forward, and all the conflicts of the Cold War — the rate was approximately 1 to 3 percent. Today, the global homicide rate is roughly 6 per 100,000 people, and the death rate from interstate war is near its lowest point in recorded history.

15% → 0.006% Death rate by violence: pre-state societies to today

The 20th century was the most violent in absolute numbers — more people died in wars than in all previous centuries combined. But the 20th century also had more people than all previous centuries combined. As a proportion of the population, the rate of death by violence was lower than in any prior era. The 21st century has been even more peaceful, by the same measure.

This data is Pinker's central contribution in The Better Angels of Our Nature. The book is 800 pages of evidence, but the core finding can be stated in one sentence: the rate at which humans kill each other has been declining for thousands of years, and the decline has accelerated in the last century.

Education: Literacy for Billions

In 1820, only 12 percent of adults worldwide could read or write. Today, the global literacy rate is 87 percent. The improvement is recent and rapid: in 1960, the global literacy rate was still only 42 percent. More than half the progress in human literacy has happened in the last 60 years.

12% → 87% Global literacy rate, 1820 to today

Primary school enrollment is now near-universal in most of the world. Secondary enrollment has risen from 26 percent globally in 1970 to 77 percent today. Tertiary enrollment — university-level education — has risen from 10 percent in 1970 to 40 percent today. An entire dimension of human capability that was available only to a small elite two centuries ago is now available to the majority of humanity.

Freedom and Democracy

In 1900, there were no countries that could be classified as democracies by the standards of political scientists today. There were a few with limited franchises — property-owning men could vote in Britain, the United States, and a handful of others — but universal suffrage was almost nowhere. Women could not vote in any country until New Zealand in 1893. By 1950, there were approximately 20 democracies. By 2000, there were approximately 90. Today, depending on the classification system used, there are between 80 and 100 electoral democracies, accounting for roughly half the world's population.

This trend is not linear. Freedom House has reported 17 consecutive years of democratic erosion — more countries are becoming less free than more free. This is real and concerning. But it does not reverse the long-term trajectory. The world of 2026, even with its democratic backsliding, is dramatically freer than the world of 1950, let alone 1900 or 1800.

Technology: The Acceleration of Human Capability

In 1980, there were approximately 250 million telephones in the world, serving a population of 4.5 billion. Today, there are over 8 billion mobile phone subscriptions, serving a population of 8 billion. More people have access to a mobile phone than have access to electricity or clean water.

Internet access has grown from 0.1 percent of the world's population in 1995 to approximately 67 percent today. In 2000, only 7 percent of people in developing countries used the internet. Today, over 50 percent do. The gap between the connected and unconnected world is narrowing, not widening.

Why This Matters

The data matters because the model of the world you carry in your head determines what you do. If you believe the world is getting worse, you make different decisions than if you believe it is getting better. You vote differently. You spend differently. You raise your children differently. You choose what to work on, what to care about, and what to contribute to.

If your model is wrong, your choices will be wrong. And the data is unambiguous: for most people, the model is wrong. The world is better than they think.

This does not mean the world is good enough. It is not. Poverty, disease, violence, oppression, and environmental destruction remain enormous problems. The point is not that progress is complete — the point is that progress is possible, visible, and measurable. A civilization that cannot see its own progress cannot build on it. A civilization that sees only decline has no reason to invest in the future.

"The person who knows the data and still chooses despair is not being realistic. They are being irrational. The person who knows the data and chooses optimism is responding appropriately to the most important facts about the world."
